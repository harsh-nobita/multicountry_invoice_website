What Is an Invoice? Everything Freelancers Need to Know

An invoice is a legally binding document that requests payment for goods or services provided. Learn the essential elements every invoice must contain and common mistakes to avoid.

Read article →

What Must an Invoice Include?

A professional invoice should contain the following elements to be legally valid and ensure prompt payment:

  • Invoice number – a unique identifier for tracking and accounting purposes.
  • Issue date and due date – when the invoice was created and when payment is expected.
  • Seller details – your full name or business name, address, and contact information.
  • Buyer details – your client's name, company, and billing address.
  • Description of goods or services – clear, itemized breakdown of what was delivered.
  • Amounts – unit price, quantity, subtotal, taxes, and grand total.
  • Payment terms – how and when to pay (bank transfer, PayPal, net-30, etc.).
  • Tax information – your GST/VAT registration number if applicable.

Invoice vs. Receipt vs. Quote

An invoice requests payment before or after work. A receipt confirms payment was received. A quote (or estimate) proposes the cost before work begins. All three are different documents.

GST vs VAT vs Sales Tax: What's the Difference?

Confused about international tax systems? This guide breaks down the major tax systems used around the world and when you need to charge them on your invoices.

Read article →

The Three Major Tax System Types

Most countries use one of three broad tax frameworks for business transactions:

SystemCountriesTypical RateNotes
VAT (Value Added Tax)EU, UK, UAE, South Africa, Russia5–25%Applied at each stage of production
GST (Goods & Services Tax)Australia, India, Canada, NZ, Singapore5–18%Similar to VAT; may have federal + state components
Sales TaxUnited States0–10.25%Applied only at point of sale; varies by state

India's GST: CGST + SGST + IGST

India has a dual GST structure. For sales within the same state, you charge CGST (Central GST) + SGST (State GST), both at half the applicable rate. For inter-state sales, you charge IGST (Integrated GST) at the full rate. Common rates are 0%, 5%, 12%, 18%, and 28%.

Canada's GST/HST/PST

Canada uses federal GST (5%) plus provincial taxes. Some provinces harmonize these into a single HST. Others have separate PST or QST (Quebec). Ontario charges 13% HST; Nova Scotia 15% HST; BC charges 5% GST + 7% PST.

When Do You Need to Register?

Most countries have a registration threshold. In Australia, you must register for GST once you earn over AUD $75,000/year. In the UK, it's £90,000. In India, it's ₹20–40 lakh depending on the state. Below the threshold, you typically don't need to charge or collect tax.

10 Proven Tips to Get Invoices Paid on Time

Late payments are a freelancer's biggest frustration. Here are proven strategies to reduce payment delays and protect your cash flow.

Read article →
  • Invoice immediately – Send the invoice the same day you complete the work. Delays on your end encourage delays on theirs.
  • Use clear due dates – "Net 30" is less urgent than "Due by June 30, 2024". Be explicit.
  • Request a deposit upfront – For large projects, ask for 25–50% before starting. This filters out unreliable clients.
  • Accept multiple payment methods – Bank transfer, PayPal, Stripe, UPI — the easier you make it to pay, the faster you get paid.
  • Include bank details on the invoice – Never make clients ask where to send the money.
  • Follow up automatically – Send a polite reminder 3 days before the due date and 1 day after.
  • Charge late fees – State a 1.5–2% monthly late fee in your terms. Even if you never enforce it, the threat works.
  • Keep invoices simple – Confusing invoices get questioned and delayed. Be clear and itemized.
  • Build a relationship – Clients pay people they like faster. A brief thank-you note goes a long way.
  • Use invoice numbers – Makes it easier for clients' accounting teams to process and track your payment.

How to Number Your Invoices (Best Practices)

A good invoice numbering system keeps your accounting organized, looks professional, and is required by tax authorities in most countries. Here's how to do it right.

Read article →

Why Invoice Numbers Matter

Invoice numbers are required by tax law in most countries. They allow you and your client to uniquely identify, reference, and audit transactions. Using sequential numbers also makes it easy to spot gaps (lost or disputed invoices).

Popular Numbering Formats

FormatExampleBest For
SequentialINV-001, INV-002Startups & freelancers
Year + Sequential2024-001, 2024-002Annual reset tracking
Year + Month + Seq2024-06-001High-volume businesses
Client Code + SeqAPPLE-001, MSFT-002Multi-client agencies
Project CodePROJ-001-INV-1Project-based billing

Rules to Follow

  • Invoice numbers must be unique — never reuse a number.
  • They must be sequential with no gaps (for tax audit compliance).
  • In many countries (EU, Australia, India) the numbering must be chronological.
  • Do not use special characters except hyphens and underscores.

Sole Trader vs. Company: How It Affects Your Invoicing

Whether you're a sole trader, partnership, or registered company affects what must appear on your invoices, your tax obligations, and liability. Here's a country-by-country breakdown.

Read article →

Sole Trader Invoicing

As a sole trader, your invoices must include your full legal name (not just a trade name) in most countries. In Australia, you should include your ABN. In the UK, you don't need to register for VAT unless you exceed the £90,000 threshold.

Company Invoicing Requirements

Registered companies typically have stricter requirements. In the UK, a limited company must show its registered company number and registered office address. In India, a Private Limited company must include its CIN. In the EU, VAT-registered businesses must show their VAT number on all B2B invoices.
